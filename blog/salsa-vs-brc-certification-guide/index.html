SALSA vs BRC Certification: Which One Does Your Food Startup Actually Need?

2026-03-04  ·  food safety, certification, SALSA, BRC, startup, UK food manufacturing

The Challenge

You've perfected your recipe. You've found a manufacturer. Now a potential retail buyer comes back with a single question that stops you in your tracks: "Are you SALSA or BRC certified?"

If you're new to UK food manufacturing, food safety certifications can feel like alphabet soup. SALSA, BRC, BRCGS, ISO 22000, FSSC 22000 — each one claims to be essential, each one costs money and time, and nobody seems to give you a straight answer about which one you actually need.

This guide cuts through the noise. Here's what each certification means, what it costs, who requires it, and — most importantly — where to start.

---

What Are These Certifications, Anyway?

Food safety certifications are independently audited frameworks that verify a manufacturer (or your own production facility) meets specific food hygiene, traceability, and process control standards. In the UK, they're the primary way retailers and foodservice buyers manage supply chain risk.

They are not the same as your basic food hygiene certificate from the council. These are serious, annual audits carried out by accredited third-party bodies.

SALSA (Safe and Local Supplier Approval)

SALSA stands for Safe and Local Supplier Approval. It was specifically designed for small and micro food businesses in the UK — think producers turning over under £5 million, cottage-scale manufacturers, and early-stage startups scaling into retail.

What it covers:

  • Food safety management systems
  • Premises and equipment hygiene
  • Pest control
  • Allergen management
  • Traceability and recall procedures

Who audits it: SALSA uses a network of approved auditors who visit your site. The audit typically takes a full day.

Cost: Expect to pay £500–£900 for the audit plus an annual membership fee of around £300–£400. Total first-year cost is typically £800–£1,300.

Who accepts it: Farm shops, delis, independent retailers, some regional supermarkets, foodservice distributors, and direct-to-consumer channels. It's increasingly accepted by Waitrose, Ocado, and some Tesco regional supplier programmes.

---

BRCGS (formerly BRC Global Standard for Food Safety)

BRCGS stands for Brand Reputation Compliance Global Standards. It's the big one — the gold standard required by most major UK supermarkets and large foodservice operators.

What it covers: Everything SALSA does, but with significantly more depth: documented food safety culture programmes, environmental monitoring, supplier approval processes, internal audit trails, and more.

Grades: BRCGS is graded A+, A, B, C, or D depending on audit performance. Most major retailers require a minimum Grade B or above.

Cost: Audit fees vary by site size and auditor body (e.g., NSF, SGS, Bureau Veritas), but budget £1,500–£3,500 per audit plus ongoing consultant support if you're preparing from scratch. Initial compliance setup can run £5,000–£15,000+ depending on how much infrastructure work is needed.

Who requires it: Tesco, Sainsbury's, Asda, Morrisons, Co-op, M&S, and most large UK foodservice operators require BRCGS as a minimum for direct suppliers.

Step-by-Step: How to Decide Which to Pursue

Step 1: Map Your Target Retailers

Before spending a penny, list your top five target stockists. Then email or call their buying or technical teams and ask directly: "What food safety certification do you require from suppliers?"

This one step saves months of wasted preparation. Don't assume — ask.

Step 2: Be Honest About Your Scale

If you're producing in a shared kitchen, a small unit, or early in your production journey, BRCGS is almost certainly not the right starting point. The documentation burden alone requires someone dedicated to quality systems — which most startups simply don't have.

SALSA was built for exactly your situation. Start there.

Step 3: Use a Co-Packer's Certification (The Smart Shortcut)

Here's the move that many food startups miss entirely: if you use a certified co-packer, their certification covers your product.

A BRCGS-certified co-packer produces your product under their certified systems. From a retailer's perspective, that box is ticked. You don't need your own certification — you need theirs.

This is one of the strongest arguments for working with a co-packer early on: you inherit their compliance infrastructure without paying for it yourself.

Step 4: If You're Manufacturing Yourself — Get SALSA First

If you're producing in-house, the path is almost always:

  1. SALSA first — builds your food safety management system, gets you into smaller retail and proves you're serious
  2. BRCGS when you're ready — pursue this when you have a dedicated site, a QA person, and confirmed orders from major multiples that require it

Step 5: Find an Approved SALSA Mentor

SALSA offers a mentoring programme where an approved mentor helps prepare your business for audit. This dramatically increases first-time pass rates and shortens prep time. Budget £500–£1,500 for mentoring support and view it as money well spent.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Chasing BRCGS too early. It's tempting to go straight for the gold standard, but the audit will fail if your systems aren't mature. A failed BRCGS audit is expensive and demoralising. Build up.

Confusing certification with registration. Your local authority registration (required for any food business in the UK) is separate from SALSA or BRCGS. Make sure you're registered with your local council before any audit.

Not reviewing allergen management. Allergen controls are scrutinised heavily in both certifications. If your facility handles any of the 14 major allergens, you need documented procedures and labelling controls in place before your audit.

Letting your certification lapse. Both certifications are annual. Retailers can — and do — delist suppliers whose certification expires. Put the renewal date in your diary the day you pass.

  2. If using a co-packer, ask for their current certification certificate before signing any agreement
  3. If manufacturing in-house, register with your local authority and start building your SALSA documentation
