How to Get Your Food Product Shelf-Ready in 90 Days

2026-03-03  ·  food startup, product launch, co-packer, packaging, timeline, entrepreneur

# How to Get Your Food Product Shelf-Ready in 90 Days

You've perfected your recipe in the kitchen. Friends and family love it. Now you're ready to turn that weekend passion into a real business. But the jump from home kitchen to retail shelf feels enormous.

Here's the truth: it doesn't have to take years. With the right approach, you can go from concept to shelf-ready in 90 days. This isn't a theoretical guide—it's a practical roadmap used by successful food startups.

The 90-Day Framework

This timeline assumes you have:

  • A tested, scalable recipe
  • Initial branding direction
  • Some budget for professional production
  • A clear target retailer in mind

If you're still experimenting with recipes, add 30-60 days. But if you're ready to execute, let's go.

Weeks 1-2: Foundation and Planning

Day 1-3: Lock your specification

  • Finalise your recipe with precise measurements by weight (not volume)
  • Document cooking temperatures, times, and critical control points
  • Create a detailed product specification sheet
  • Note: Changes after this point get expensive

Day 4-7: Regulatory groundwork

  • Register your food business with your local authority (legally required 28 days before trading)
  • Research your product category's specific regulations (allergen labelling, nutritional claims, etc.)
  • Decide on your business structure (sole trader vs limited company)
  • Get food hygiene training (Level 2 minimum, Level 3 recommended)

Day 8-14: Co-packer research

  • Create a shortlist of 5-10 potential manufacturers
  • Request quotes with minimum order quantities (MOQs)
  • Ask about lead times, capacity, and their certification status
  • Check reviews or request references
  • Narrow to 3 for site visits

Weeks 3-4: Co-Packer Selection and Packaging Design

Week 3: Site visits and selection

  • Visit your shortlisted co-packers
  • Assess cleanliness, organisation, and professionalism
  • Ask about their experience with your product type
  • Review their certification (SALSA minimum, BRC for major retailers)
  • Check their production schedule alignment with your needs
  • Make your decision and sign contracts

Week 4: Packaging design sprint

  • Work with a designer on label artwork
  • Include all required elements: ingredients, allergens, nutritional info, best before date, storage instructions, weight, manufacturer address, lot code
  • Get legal review if making health or nutritional claims
  • Request 3D mockups before finalising
  • Send artwork to your packaging supplier

Critical packaging considerations:

  • Shelf life requirements (affects barrier properties)
  • Retailer specifications (some have strict guidelines)
  • Sustainability credentials (increasingly important)
  • Cost per unit at your initial volume

Weeks 5-8: Production Preparation

Week 5-6: First production run planning

  • Finalise your production schedule with the co-packer
  • Confirm ingredient sourcing (they may use their suppliers or yours)
  • Order packaging materials
  • Plan your initial batch size (start smaller than you think—300-500 units is common)

Week 7: Label compliance check

  • Submit labels for regulatory review if uncertain
  • Verify all mandatory information is present
  • Check font sizes meet minimum requirements
  • Ensure allergen information is accurate and prominent

Week 8: Dry runs and corrections

  • Conduct a trial production run if possible
  • Test packaging durability and appearance
  • Verify shelf life claims with testing
  • Make any final adjustments to the process

Weeks 9-12: Production, Testing, and Launch

Week 9: First production run

  • Be present for the first run if the co-packer allows it
  • Document the process
  • Take photos for marketing
  • Collect samples for testing and pitching

Week 10: Quality assurance

  • Conduct shelf-life testing (accelerated if time is short)
  • Get third-party microbiological testing if required
  • Check packaging integrity
  • Verify labelling accuracy on finished product

Week 11: Sales preparation

  • Create your sales pitch and materials
  • Contact your target retailers
  • Prepare delivery logistics
  • Set up inventory tracking

Week 12: Launch

  • Deliver to your first retailer
  • Monitor initial sales and feedback
  • Be ready for rapid iteration

The Co-Packer Selection Process: A Deeper Dive

Choosing the right manufacturing partner can make or break your timeline. Here's how to evaluate them:

Essential questions to ask:

  1. What's your minimum order quantity?
  2. What's your typical lead time from order to delivery?
  3. What certifications do you hold?
  4. Have you made products similar to mine?
  5. Can you source ingredients, or must I provide them?
  6. What are your payment terms?
  7. Can I visit during production?

Red flags:

  • Reluctance to answer questions
  • No food safety certification
  • Unwilling to provide references
  • Facility appears disorganised or unclean
  • Significantly cheaper than competitors (there's usually a reason)

Green flags:

  • Clear, documented processes
  • Willingness to work with startups
  • Good communication
  • Experience with your product category
  • Reasonable but not suspiciously low pricing

Regulatory Requirements You Can't Skip

Legal essentials:

  • Business registration with local authority
  • Food hygiene training
  • Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points (HACCP) plan
  • Traceability system (one step back, one step forward)
  • Allergen management procedures

Label requirements:

  • Product name
  • Ingredient list (descending order by weight)
  • Allergen highlighting
  • Net quantity
  • Best before or use-by date
  • Storage conditions
  • Manufacturer name and address
  • Country of origin (if required)
  • Nutritional information (mandatory for most products)

Optional but recommended:

  • Product liability insurance
  • Food safety certification (SALSA or BRC)
  • Organic certification (if applicable)
  • Trademark protection

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

Recipe scalability issues Home recipes often don't scale directly. Flavours can change, textures can shift, and cooking times vary at volume. Always test at production scale before committing.

Underestimating packaging costs Packaging often costs more than the product itself at small volumes. Get firm quotes early and factor this into your pricing.

Regulatory surprises Don't assume your label is compliant. Get it checked. Fines for mislabelling can destroy a startup.

Production delays Build buffer time into your plans. Co-packers get busy, ingredients arrive late, and equipment breaks down. Having a 2-week buffer could save your launch.

The Day 90 Checklist

By day 90, you should have:

  • [ ] Signed co-packer agreement
  • [ ] Completed first production run
  • [ ] Compliant packaging and labelling
  • [ ] Food safety systems documented
  • [ ] Business registration complete
  • [ ] Initial inventory ready
  • [ ] First retailer committed or already stocking
  • [ ] Traceability system operational

Final Thoughts

Ninety days is aggressive but achievable. The key is parallel execution—don't wait for one task to finish before starting another. While your packaging is being designed, select your co-packer. While waiting for production, prepare your sales materials.

Remember: perfect is the enemy of launched. Your first version doesn't need to be flawless—it needs to be safe, legal, and good enough to test the market. You can iterate once you're selling.

The food industry rewards those who move decisively. Start today. Launch in 90 days. Learn from real customers.
